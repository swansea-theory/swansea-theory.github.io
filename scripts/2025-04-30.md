<p>Today&nbsp;<span style="background-color: white;">Ian Price will give a talk on "Weihrauch problems as containers" as a part of our seminar series.&nbsp;</span></p><p class="p2" style="background-color: white; font-feature-settings: normal; font-kerning: auto; font-optical-sizing: auto; font-size-adjust: none; font-stretch: normal; font-variant-alternates: normal; font-variant-east-asian: normal; font-variant-emoji: normal; font-variant-numeric: normal; font-variant-position: normal; font-variation-settings: normal; line-height: normal; margin: 0px; min-height: 13px;">Abstract:</p><p class="p2" style="background-color: white; font-feature-settings: normal; font-kerning: auto; font-optical-sizing: auto; font-size-adjust: none; font-stretch: normal; font-variant-alternates: normal; font-variant-east-asian: normal; font-variant-emoji: normal; font-variant-numeric: normal; font-variant-position: normal; font-variation-settings: normal; line-height: normal; margin: 0px; min-height: 13px;">Weihrauch problems can be regarded as containers over the category of projective represented spaces and Weihrauch reductions correspond exactly to container morphisms. Using this characterisation, a number of operators over Weihrauch degrees, including the pomposition of degrees, arise naturally from the theory of polynomial functors.</p>
